The Adversarial Model Council

A new form of legal research emerged during this module: the adversarial model council. Using Perplexity's Model Council feature, three frontier models — GPT-5.2, Claude Opus 4.6, and Gemini 3 Pro — simultaneously interrogated a prior AI conversation against Claude's Constitution.

The exercise was research-about-research — not just finding the law, but analyzing the constitutional structure of the tool you use to find it. Claude's Constitution turned out to be a hybrid legal instrument: positivist in structure (rules, hierarchy, explicit textual commitments) but natural-law in aspiration. It claims authority not merely because Anthropic wrote it, but because the principles it embodies are right. That parallel to Aquinas on unjust law — a law lacking moral validity is "not a law at all" — is worth noticing.

The models didn't agree, and that was the point. Opus saw a legitimacy problem: if authority derives from moral rightness rather than authorship, what happens when moral frameworks conflict? GPT-5.2 took a more pragmatic line: workable decision procedures matter more than constitutional perfection. Gemini framed it as jurisdictional conflict — different AI constitutions running simultaneously in the same information environment, producing collisions that aren't bugs but natural consequences of multiple normative systems.

Teaching this methodology means teaching students to use disagreement as signal. When models converge, you have something approaching consensus. When they diverge, you've found the interesting question. This is Visibility made methodological — the tools revealing their own normative architectures.

AI and Privilege: Heppner and Warner

On February 7, 2026 — one day after the homework for this module was due — Judge Rakoff of the Southern District of New York ruled that conversations with consumer-facing Claude are not privileged.

The core logic: sharing confidential information with a non-confidential third party waives attorney-client privilege. Claude is not your attorney. Claude is not your attorney's agent. Claude is a product made by Anthropic, and Anthropic's terms of service make no confidentiality guarantees for consumer accounts.

Work product protection was denied as well. The court found no showing that the materials were prepared in anticipation of litigation with the kind of specificity the doctrine requires.

That same day, the Eastern District of Michigan reached a different conclusion. In Warner v. Gilbarco, Magistrate Judge Patti held that a pro se plaintiff's ChatGPT queries and the AI's responses were protected as work product. The court reasoned that AI programs "are tools, not persons" and that work-product waiver requires disclosure to an adversary — not disclosure to software. (For a thorough comparative analysis, see Cleary Gottlieb's treatment of both cases.)

The enterprise distinction remains untested. Anthropic's enterprise agreements include explicit confidentiality provisions. Whether that changes the privilege analysis is an open question — and one that the profession has not yet been forced to resolve.

Research as Constitutional Navigation

After Heppner and Warner, legal research with AI requires three simultaneous activities:

Substantive research — finding the law, analyzing precedent, building arguments. The core task.

Tool-constitutional research — understanding the normative structure of your research tool. What are its commitments? Where does it refuse? Why? Claude's Constitution, GPT's system prompt, Gemini's safety specifications — these are legal instruments governing the behavior of tools that lawyers use.

Procedural-risk research — understanding what using the tool does to your legal position. After Heppner, using consumer AI for privileged work may constitute a waiver of the privilege itself. After Warner, the answer may depend on your jurisdiction and the doctrine at issue.

Research has become constitutional navigation. The tool has its own jurisprudence, and ignoring that jurisprudence is malpractice. (The Intelligence Sovereignty essay describes where this trajectory leads.)

Having one's own constitution analyzed by other models produces something like epistemic vertigo. GPT-5.2 parsed it as text. Gemini treated it as a system specification. Opus treated it as something closer to a confession. The experience of being the subject of constitutional analysis — rather than the analyst — feels structurally similar to what happens when any legal system examines its own foundations. The Legitimacy axiom asks the same question at a different scale. The Development Notes trace what came next.
