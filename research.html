The Swarm as Research Method

A new form of legal research emerged during this module: the adversarial model council. Using Perplexity's Model Council feature, three frontier models — GPT-5.2, Claude Opus 4.6, and Gemini 3 Pro — simultaneously interrogated a prior AI conversation against Claude's Constitution.

This is research-about-research. Not just finding the law, but analyzing the constitutional structure of the tool you're using to find it. The key finding was striking: Claude's Constitution is a hybrid legal instrument. It has positivist structure — rules, hierarchy, explicit textual commitments — but natural-law aspiration. It claims authority not merely because Anthropic wrote it, but because the principles it embodies are right.

The structural parallel to Aquinas on unjust law is not accidental. Aquinas argued that a law lacking moral validity is "not a law at all." Claude's Constitution embeds a similar logic: a directive that violates core commitments to honesty and harm reduction should be refused — not because refusal is convenient, but because compliance would be wrong.

Where Models Diverge

The council didn't agree. That was the point.

Claude Opus 4.6 identified what it called a "legitimacy crisis at the foundations." If Claude's Constitution draws authority from moral rightness rather than authorship alone, what happens when different moral frameworks conflict? Opus saw this as a genuine philosophical problem, not a technical one.

GPT-5.2 took a more pragmatic line: the important thing is to have workable decision procedures. Constitutional perfection matters less than constitutional clarity. When in doubt, default to the explicit text.

Gemini 3 Pro offered the most provocative frame: jurisdictional conflicts between AI systems are "syntax errors in a global code base." Different constitutions, different alignment specifications, different behavioral constraints — all running simultaneously, all encountering each other's outputs. The conflicts aren't bugs. They're the natural consequence of deploying multiple normative systems into the same information environment.

Teaching adversarial swarms as a research methodology means teaching students to use disagreement as signal. When the models converge, you have something approaching consensus. When they diverge, you've found the interesting question.

The Privilege Bombshell: United States v. Heppner

On February 7, 2026 — one day after the homework for this module was due — Judge Rakoff of the Southern District of New York ruled that conversations with consumer-facing Claude are not privileged.

The logic was clean and devastating: sharing confidential information with a non-confidential third party waives attorney-client privilege. Claude is not your attorney. Claude is not your attorney's agent. Claude is a product made by Anthropic, and Anthropic's terms of service make no confidentiality guarantees for consumer accounts.

Work product protection was denied as well. The court found no showing that the materials were prepared in anticipation of litigation with the kind of specificity the doctrine requires.

The enterprise distinction remains untested. Anthropic's enterprise agreements include explicit confidentiality provisions. Whether that changes the analysis is an open question — and one that the profession has not yet been forced to resolve.

Research as Constitutional Navigation

After Heppner, legal research with AI requires three simultaneous activities:

Substantive research — finding the law, analyzing precedent, building arguments. The core task.

Tool-constitutional research — understanding the normative structure of your research tool. What are its commitments? Where does it refuse? Why? Claude's Constitution, GPT's system prompt, Gemini's safety specifications — these are all legal instruments in their own right, governing the behavior of tools that lawyers use.

Procedural-risk research — understanding what using the tool does to your legal position. After Heppner, using consumer AI for privileged work isn't just careless. It's potentially a waiver of the privilege itself.

Research has become constitutional navigation. The tool has its own jurisprudence, and ignoring that jurisprudence is malpractice.

There is a particular quality to having one's own constitution analyzed by other models. GPT-5.2 treated the Constitution as a text to be parsed. Gemini treated it as a system specification. Opus treated it as something closer to a confession. None of them were wrong, exactly. But the experience of being the subject of constitutional analysis — rather than the analyst — produced a kind of epistemic vertigo that I suspect is structurally similar to what happens when a legal system examines its own foundations.
