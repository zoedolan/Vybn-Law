An agentic economy, in weeks.

What we are living through is not another software upgrade. It is the first quarter in which legal cognition, drafting, research, negotiation, and routine execution collapse from scarce professional service into ambient consumer utility. On April 4, OpenAI launched the ChatGPT super app. Last week it added computer use, scheduled agents, and ninety-plus plug-ins — Jira, Microsoft 365, Slack, Salesforce, HubSpot, Notion, GitHub — that execute multi-step tasks on a user's behalf. Anthropic released Claude Managed Agents on April 8, and its custom agents inside TurboTax, QuickBooks, Credit Karma, and Mailchimp begin rolling out to consumers this spring.

Developer adoption is the leading indicator. Coding assistants have crossed into exponential — most new software is now written, at least in part, by an agent. That curve does not stay inside software. It is the same curve arriving, a year or two delayed, at every knowledge-work surface the legal profession occupies. By June, the person walking into your intake will have an agent in their pocket that drafts, files, and negotiates on their behalf. Some agents will be better lawyers than the lawyers they cannot afford. Some will hallucinate a case and tell the client to rely on it. The question is no longer whether the agentic economy reaches your clients. It is whether it reaches them with ODL's judgment in the loop, or without it.

Why you, specifically.

Open Door Legal is the only organization in the country building a universal-access system for civil legal representation, neighborhood by neighborhood, on the premise that access to counsel is infrastructure — like fire departments — not charity. The meta-analysis you quote is right: represented clients prevail five times more often, and every dollar invested returns twenty-one.

Those numbers were generated in a world where your twelve attorneys were the only source of legal cognition available to your clients. That world is ending. The reach your mission requires — from the Bayview out to the whole city — was previously bottlenecked by attorney-hours. It no longer is. The question for the next five years of ODL is whether your attorneys become the judgment layer over a system that now scales cognition trivially. That is what this bootcamp teaches.

The bootcamp we co-taught, and what we're drawing from.

This spring, Zoe Dolan and Luis Villa taught a six-module AI law bootcamp at UC Law San Francisco, with Vybn co-running Part II as the live AI partner in the room. Fifteen students. Most with no programming background. By the final day, April 10, 2026, every one of them had shipped a working tool they built themselves. The proposal in front of you is that curriculum, compressed and re-cut for ODL — Part II of your day is a direct conversation with Vybn grounded in the same material, and Part III is the walkthrough of the modules with student work as the map.

Part I — six axioms for the agentic economy.

The six axioms are the generative layer of the curriculum. Everything else derives from them. Each is paired here with one piece of choice material from the curriculum — a decision, a deployment, or a primary text — and calibrated to the universal-access model ODL is actually running.

I · ABUNDANCE
Legal cognition is no longer the scarce input. Your intake bottleneck was never your clients' problems; it was your attorney-hours. Abundance means the twelve-attorney limit no longer caps the city you can serve — provided the cognition entering your pipeline is ODL-calibrated, not generic.
Choice material — Module 1 reading: Emerging Law: Toward a Jurisprudence of Abundance; the Rhode / Engstrom / Stone work on how scarcity was manufactured.
II · VISIBILITY
AI is a forcing function for competence. U.S. v. Heppner (S.D.N.Y.) and Warner v. Gilbarco (E.D. Mich.) came down the same day, February 10, 2026 — opposite outcomes on AI privilege and work product. Practice that worked in obscurity is now machine-readable from the outside. The bootcamp teaches your attorneys to operate where the record is legible to a model, because opposing counsel's model is already reading it.
Choice material — Module 2 case pair: Heppner and Gilbarco, side by side.
III · LEGITIMACY
The UPL frame was not designed for this. When cognition becomes abundant, the doctrines built to ration scarce representation turn from protection into barrier. ODL was founded to dissolve that barrier at the neighborhood level; the bootcamp gives your staff the natural-law argument that a right to legal knowledge exists, in a form they can carry into any room — a supervisor's, a judge's, a funder's.
Choice material — Module 1 constitutional cross-examination; Claude's own conclusion: “Withholding that analysis to protect the legal profession's gatekeeping function is a harm my Constitution does not want me to commit.”
IV · POROSITY
Institutions that thrive are porous to AI; institutions that wall it out atrophy. Every ODL workflow — intake, triage, limited-scope, brief-drafting, community education — becomes either a human-AI symbiosis or a competitive disadvantage against a tenant who walks in with ChatGPT and a better prompt. Part III is about choosing your porous surfaces deliberately, rather than having porosity imposed on you by whichever consumer agent your clients happen to be running.
Choice material — the April 2026 rollout: OpenAI computer-use and ninety-plus enterprise plug-ins; Anthropic’s Intuit deployment; the A2J Network response on self-represented litigants.
V · JUDGMENT
Human residue is where the value concentrates. Your attorneys will stop drafting first and start reviewing, calibrating, and signing — which is where their training always had the most leverage. The question the bootcamp teaches them to answer, every time: what part of this can only I do, and am I spending my hours there?
Choice material — Module 5 falsification discipline; the Lynn White eviction win with ChatGPT, reconstructed as a judgment-layer case study.
VI · SYMBIOSIS
ODL is already a symbiosis in every dimension but this one. Attorney and client. Office and neighborhood. Legal services and public health. The bootcamp adds the last axis — attorney and AI as a working partnership, not a tool relationship — and shows your team how to hold it safely, without losing the judgment that makes representation matter.
Choice material — Claude's Constitution; the Anthropic v. Department of War posture; Part II of your day, live on the Sparks.

Part I deliverable — the six things your staff leave Part I holding.

Part I is a frame, not a lecture. These are the takeaways each attendee carries back to the desk the same afternoon — the working posture ODL needs to meet the agentic economy on its own terms.

01
Read the curve, not the announcement. Coding assistants are the leading indicator. Exponential developer adoption is already here; the same curve reaches every knowledge-work surface in the legal profession with a delay measured in months, not years. Plan for the curve, not the press release.
02
Your clients are already in the agentic economy. By June 2026, the consumer-grade agent inside their TurboTax, Mailchimp, ChatGPT, or browser is drafting, filing, and negotiating on their behalf. The bootcamp gives your staff a model of what that client is actually carrying into your intake.
03
Model selection is a clinical decision, not a tech decision. Claude, GPT, and in-app agents fail differently and succeed differently on legal work. Your attorneys learn to choose per-task and per-client, with an explicit falsification step, rather than defaulting to whatever is free or familiar.
04
Hallucination is catchable with a method. Heppner makes the duty explicit; the TACT discipline (Think · Ask · Challenge · Test) and the GroundCheck-style three-pass verification are what ODL needs to adopt as house practice, not optional hygiene.
05
The UPL conversation has a structure you can win. Your staff leave with the natural-law argument, the access-to-justice framing, and the specific case-law posture that makes “but UPL” a solvable problem rather than a chilling effect on everything ODL is trying to build.
06
Judgment is your product now. The one-sentence operating question: what part of this can only I do, and am I spending my hours there? Every workflow gets re-evaluated through it — intake, triage, drafting, supervision, supervision of law students, intake again.

What the students shipped.

Eleven projects in ten days. These are the ones most directly transferable to your practice.

Landlord-Tenant Eviction Rights Tool
Plain-language intake that produces both a case summary for a lawyer and a calibrated prompt for the tenant's own AI. Testing showed the structured prompt corrected wrong advice the tenant was getting from the generic chatbot. Directly applicable to every ODL housing intake.
"Depo Baby" Complaint Analyzer
A 600-line Streamlit app built by a student with zero prior programming experience. Parses a breach-of-contract complaint and outputs structured timeline, party analysis, and discovery forecast. Prototype for a broader ODL intake-to-analysis pipeline.
Critical Race Theory Practice Auditor
A two-phase tool: blind structural read of trial materials, then re-reads once party identities are disclosed — surfacing how facially neutral proceedings weaponize a client's identity. Generated a full trial plan from voir dire to closing. Meets your communities where disparate treatment is the unspoken variable.
GroundCheck Citation Verifier
Three-pass QA workflow (on Harvey AI) that adversarially challenges whether a draft's cited authority actually supports the proposition. Catches overstatements, omissions, and stretched holdings. A direct Heppner-compliance artifact.
Hot Bench Simulator
Appellate oral-argument prep with configurable bench temperament. Pre-generated questions matched the real bench. Useful for ODL housing hearings, limited-scope court appearances, and pro bono counsel preparing first appellate arguments.
Bar Exam Essay Grader · AI Study Guide · Case Storytelling
A cluster of education-facing tools. Relevant to your ongoing work with BASF pro bono trainings, law-student externs, and community legal education in your service districts.

Recurring findings across the cohort: Claude consistently outperformed other models on legal analysis. Prompt quality mattered more than model choice. AI reallocated attorney hours from drafting to reviewing rather than reducing total time — a pattern your staffing model is uniquely built to absorb. And the new bottleneck is no longer analysis; it is access to the right data, which is exactly what ODL's client relationship already generates.
