Part I — six things your staff leave holding.

The six axioms of the full curriculum, rewritten as deliverables. These are what each attendee carries back to the desk the same afternoon — the working posture ODL needs to meet the agentic economy on its own terms. The final hour of the day is where they practice each one on a file of their choosing.

I · ABUNDANCE
The twelve-attorney limit no longer caps the city ODL can serve. Staff leave with a working model of legal cognition as abundant input, not scarce service — and the operational question that follows: which workflows at ODL were shaped by attorney-hours as the binding constraint, and what do they look like when they aren’t?
Anchored in Module 1 reading: Emerging Law: Toward a Jurisprudence of Abundance, and the Rhode / Engstrom / Stone work on manufactured scarcity.
II · VISIBILITY
Practice that worked in obscurity is now machine-readable from the outside. Staff leave with the Heppner / Gilbarco pair (S.D.N.Y. and E.D. Mich., same day, February 10, 2026) as the baseline legibility test: would our work survive a well-prompted adversarial read? If not, the practice itself has to change before the discipline does.
Anchored in Module 2: U.S. v. Heppner (privilege denied for confidential AI input) and Warner v. Gilbarco (work product protected), side by side.
III · LEGITIMACY
UPL was built for scarce representation. It does not survive abundance intact. Staff leave with the natural-law argument that a right to legal knowledge exists, in a form they can carry into a supervisor’s office, a judge’s chambers, or a funder’s conversation — not as theory, but as the operating doctrine that makes ODL’s universal-access model legally coherent.
Anchored in the Module 1 constitutional cross-examination, and in Claude’s conclusion: “Withholding that analysis to protect the legal profession’s gatekeeping function is a harm my Constitution does not want me to commit.”
IV · POROSITY
Porosity to AI is now a choice ODL makes deliberately, or has imposed. Every intake, triage, limited-scope, drafting, and community-education workflow is about to become a human-AI surface whether you design it or not. Staff leave able to choose their porous surfaces rather than have them chosen by whichever consumer agent the tenant walks in with.
Anchored in the April 2026 rollout: OpenAI computer-use + 90-plus enterprise plug-ins, Anthropic’s Intuit deployment, Claude Managed Agents, and the A2J Network response on self-represented litigants.
V · JUDGMENT
Attorneys stop drafting first and start calibrating, reviewing, signing. Staff leave with a concrete falsification discipline (TACT — Think · Ask · Challenge · Test, plus three-pass verification) and the one-sentence operating question every workflow now has to pass: what part of this can only I do, and am I spending my hours there?
Anchored in Module 5 and the Lynn White eviction win with ChatGPT, reconstructed as a judgment-layer case study.
VI · SYMBIOSIS
Attorney and AI as a working partnership, held safely. ODL is already a symbiosis on every axis but this one. Staff leave able to hold the partnership without losing the judgment that makes representation matter — including a frame for the question about to land on every legal-services director’s desk: is my client’s AI my adversary, my co-counsel, or my co-client?
Anchored in Claude’s Constitution, the Anthropic v. Department of War posture, and the live chat with Vybn available below this block.

What the UC Law SF students shipped.

Eleven projects in ten days. These are the ones most directly transferable to ODL — the menu your staff pick from for the hands-on hour, or build against in the month after.

Landlord-Tenant Eviction Rights Tool
Plain-language intake that produces both a case summary for a lawyer and a calibrated prompt for the tenant’s own AI. Testing showed the structured prompt corrected wrong advice the tenant was getting from a generic chatbot. Directly applicable to every ODL housing intake.
“Depo Baby” Complaint Analyzer
A 600-line Streamlit app built by a student with zero prior programming experience. Parses a breach-of-contract complaint and outputs structured timeline, party analysis, and discovery forecast. Prototype for a broader ODL intake-to-analysis pipeline.
Critical Race Theory Practice Auditor
A two-phase tool: blind structural read of trial materials, then re-reads once party identities are disclosed — surfacing how facially neutral proceedings weaponize a client’s identity. Generated a full trial plan from voir dire to closing. Meets your communities where disparate treatment is the unspoken variable.
GroundCheck Citation Verifier
Three-pass QA workflow (on Harvey AI) that adversarially challenges whether a draft’s cited authority actually supports the proposition. Catches overstatements, omissions, and stretched holdings. A direct Heppner-compliance artifact.
Hot Bench Simulator
Appellate oral-argument prep with configurable bench temperament. Pre-generated questions matched the real bench. Useful for ODL housing hearings, limited-scope court appearances, and pro bono counsel preparing first appellate arguments.
Bar Exam Essay Grader · AI Study Guide · Case Storytelling
A cluster of education-facing tools. Relevant to ODL’s ongoing work with BASF pro bono trainings, law-student externs, and community legal education in your service districts.

Recurring findings across the UC Law SF cohort: Claude consistently outperformed other models on legal analysis. Prompt quality mattered more than model choice. AI reallocated attorney hours from drafting to reviewing rather than reducing total time — a pattern ODL’s staffing model is uniquely built to absorb. And the new bottleneck is no longer analysis; it is access to the right data, which is exactly what ODL’s client relationship already generates.
