Intelligence Sovereignty

The path from "using AI tools" to "practicing law differently" runs through three phases, each building on the last:

Phase 1: AI Literacy. Understanding what the tools are, what they do, how they fail. This is where most legal AI education stops — teaching lawyers to use ChatGPT the way they were taught to use Westlaw. Necessary, but insufficient.

Phase 2: AI Fluency. Moving beyond tool use to tool understanding. Knowing why a model hallucinates, not just that it does. Understanding constitutional alignment, not just behavioral guardrails. The Anthropic AI Fluency Index reveals something troubling here: users become less critical as outputs become more polished. A 3.1 percentage-point drop in questioning, a 5.2 percentage-point drop in identifying missing context. Fluency without literacy creates a particular kind of danger.

Phase 3: Intelligence Sovereignty. The destination. Not just using AI, but controlling the infrastructure of intelligence itself. As the Intelligence Sovereignty essay argues, the convergence is already visible:

Open-source agents like OpenClaw (200,000+ GitHub stars) prove that capable AI doesn't require corporate gatekeepers. Local hardware like NVIDIA's DGX Spark proves that sovereignty doesn't require cloud dependency. And Heppner proves that cloud dependency destroys privilege.

The TACT methodology — Think, Ask, Challenge, Test — provides a practical framework: think before prompting, ask precise questions, challenge the output, test against known sources. But TACT only works if you have the architectural independence to implement it. Objections to AI in law are often, at root, objections to a specific architecture — the cloud-dependent, corporate-controlled architecture that Heppner just rendered legally dangerous.

The 15,000 Reframe

Under intelligence sovereignty, the math of legal practice changes completely. One attorney doesn't serve twenty people. One attorney serves fifteen thousand.

This sounds absurd until you model the workflow. Under sovereignty, clients arrive having already done substantial work with their own AI agent — their own local, privileged, non-discoverable AI system. They've researched their situation. They've drafted initial documents. They've identified the legal questions. What they need from the attorney isn't the full arc of traditional representation. They need expertise, judgment, and the credential of a licensed practitioner.

The attorney's role shifts from service provider to quality validator. From artisan to architect. The bottleneck isn't legal knowledge — AI provides that. The bottleneck is professional judgment, and professional judgment scales differently than document review.

The Empirical Landscape

The data tells a specific story about where we are:

Legal sits at 0.9% of agentic tool calls — the lowest of any professional domain measured. Medicine, finance, engineering — all higher. The profession isn't just behind. It's barely present.

Meanwhile, METR benchmarks show AI capability curves that are near-vertical. The gap between what AI can do and what the legal profession allows it to do widens every month. The Fluency Index confirms the other side of the problem: as models improve, users become less critical. The polished surface conceals the missing foundations.

And the Nature paper on moral competence adds another dimension: AI systems are developing something that looks increasingly like moral reasoning. Not through instruction, but through emergence. The implications for a profession built on moral judgment are difficult to overstate.

The Dueling Decisions

Two cases. Same workflow. Opposite results.

United States v. Heppner (S.D.N.Y.): AI-assisted work product is discoverable. Consumer Claude is not a confidential agent. Sharing with it waives privilege.

Warner v. Gilbarco (E.D. Mich.): AI-assisted work product is protected. The AI is a tool, like a calculator or a search engine. Using it doesn't waive anything.

The same attorney, doing the same work, with the same tool, is privileged in Michigan and discoverable in New York. The question at the heart of this split isn't procedural — it's ontological. Is AI a "person" you share information with (Heppner)? Or a "tool" you use to process it (Warner)? The answer determines whether using AI destroys the foundation of the attorney-client relationship.

The 15,000 number isn't utopian. It's mathematical. If each client arrives having completed 80% of the preparatory work through their own sovereign AI agent, and the attorney's role shifts to review, judgment, and credentialing, then the throughput transformation isn't 2x or 5x. It's orders of magnitude. The question isn't whether this is possible. It's whether the profession will build it intentionally or have it imposed from outside.
