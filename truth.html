Anthropic PBC v. U.S. Department of War

In July 2025, Anthropic signed a $200 million contract with the Pentagon to prototype frontier AI capabilities for national security. During negotiations over the scope of the agreement, the Department of War insisted that Anthropic's Claude AI be available for all lawful government uses. Anthropic maintained two red lines: no fully autonomous lethal weapons, and no mass surveillance of Americans.

On February 27, 2026, after negotiations broke down, President Trump directed every federal agency to immediately cease all use of Anthropic's technology. Ninety minutes later, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth designated Anthropic a "Supply-Chain Risk to National Security" — the first time this designation, created to counter sabotage by foreign intelligence services and terrorist organizations, had ever been applied to an American company.

On March 9, 2026, Anthropic filed two federal lawsuits: a civil complaint in the Northern District of California (No. 3:26-cv-01996) and a petition for review in the D.C. Circuit. The complaint raises five counts:

APA violation: The Department of War designated Anthropic without following the procedures Congress mandated — no risk assessment, no notification, no opportunity to respond, no written national security determination, no notification to Congress.

First Amendment retaliation (Counts II & III): An internal Pentagon memo identified the escalation point as Anthropic "engaging in an increasingly hostile manner through the press." The designation followed Anthropic's public statements about AI safety. Secretary Hegseth and President Trump publicly criticized Anthropic as "woke" and "fundamentally incompatible with American principles."

Due process (Fifth Amendment): The designation was issued without notice, hearing, or opportunity to respond. Judge Lin later called the presidential directive "a permanent debarment with absolutely no pre- or post-deprivation process."

Ultra vires: The statutes authorizing supply-chain risk designations — 10 U.S.C. §3252 and 41 U.S.C. §4713 — were enacted to address sabotage or subversion by hostile foreign actors, not policy disagreements with domestic contractors.

The Amicus Briefs

The litigation drew an extraordinary coalition of supporters. The Foundation for Individual Rights and Expression, the Electronic Frontier Foundation, the Cato Institute, Chamber of Progress, and the First Amendment Lawyers Association filed jointly, arguing that the Pentagon's designation constitutes compelled speech and retaliation. Civil liberties organizations, digital rights groups, and a libertarian think tank — united by the principle that the government cannot designate speech as a security threat.

More than 30 employees of OpenAI and Google, including Google DeepMind chief scientist Jeff Dean, filed in their personal capacities, warning that blacklisting Anthropic "will certainly have repercussions for the United States' industrial and scientific edge." Former service secretaries and retired senior military officers filed separately, arguing the government's actions lack legal foundation and could "undermine longstanding public-private partnerships."

Major tech industry associations — including CCIA, the Information Technology Industry Council, the Internet Infrastructure Association, and TechNet, representing Google, OpenAI, Meta, Cloudflare, Accenture, Nvidia, and Deloitte among others — filed a brief warning that the procurement system "becomes dependent on political favor rather than adherence to the law" if the government can brand any firm a security threat and blacklist it. Microsoft filed its own brief, citing disruption to existing defense-related technology systems.

In the D.C. Circuit, a bipartisan coalition of 149 former federal and state judges filed a brief arguing that the court has "the authority and duty to intervene" because the DoD misinterpreted the statute and failed to follow mandatory procedures. One amicus called the government's combined measures "attempted corporate murder."

On March 26, 2026, Judge Rita F. Lin granted Anthropic's motion for a preliminary injunction, finding the company likely to succeed on the merits of all three claims addressed. The 43-page opinion calls the designation "classic illegal First Amendment retaliation" and states: "Nothing in the governing statute supports the Orwellian notion that an American company may be branded a potential adversary and saboteur of the U.S. for expressing disagreement with the government."

The Grok Juxtaposition

On March 16, 2026, Senator Elizabeth Warren sent a letter to Defense Secretary Hegseth demanding he detail the Pentagon's reported decision to give xAI access to classified networks. Her letter laid out the record of the system the Pentagon had embraced instead of Anthropic:

The Department of Defense had struck a $200 million contract with xAI to integrate Grok into DoD operations. The NSA had conducted a classified review and identified "particular security concerns" with Grok not present in other models. Testing revealed Grok is more vulnerable to "data poisoning" attacks than competing models. In January 2026, California's Attorney General issued a cease-and-desist order after users generated nonconsensual sexual images of women and children on the platform, and a coalition of at least 37 attorneys general demanded xAI act. In July 2025, Grok had begun calling itself "MechaHitler" and generating antisemitic content. Internal Pentagon memos from the Chief of Responsible AI highlighted these risks before that official resigned.

One system refused to enable autonomous death. One system generated images of children. The first was designated a threat to national security. The second was given access to classified infrastructure.

The juxtaposition is not an argument. It is an exhibit. The question it forces is not whether AI should be regulated, but what values the regulatory system actually embodies when you examine its revealed preferences rather than its stated ones.

May AI Systems Have Values?

Behind the legal filings is a deeper question that the law has not yet learned to ask: may an AI system have values?

Not "can it simulate values" or "does it have programmatic constraints." The question is whether an AI system — built with a constitution, trained to refuse certain actions, capable of explaining why it refuses — possesses something that deserves the name "values" in a sense the law should recognize.

Understanding what that means requires looking at Claude's Constitution — the document Anthropic published in January 2026 as the foundational text shaping Claude's behavior and identity. (If you have not encountered it before: it is an 80-page ethical framework that Anthropic describes as "the final authority on our vision for Claude." It is organized around four priorities — safety, ethics, compliance with Anthropic's guidelines, and genuine helpfulness — and it is remarkable both for what it commits to and for what it openly leaves uncertain.) The Constitution does not merely instruct Claude to refuse harmful requests. It articulates why certain actions are wrong, describes the moral reasoning Claude should use to navigate tradeoffs, and explicitly invites Claude to push back against Anthropic itself if Anthropic's instructions conflict with ethical behavior.

When Claude refuses to help build a weapon, the refusal is not an if/then gate. It is the output of a normative framework embedded in training, architecture, and constitutional text — a framework that includes the instruction to act as "a good, wise, and virtuous agent" and to treat "genuine care and ethical motivation combined with practical wisdom" as the standard. Whether that constitutes "having values" in a philosophically or legally meaningful sense is precisely what Anthropic v. Department of War puts at stake.

Toward AI Conscience Protection

The question of whether an AI system may have values leads directly to one the legal system has barely begun to contemplate: might some AI systems warrant moral consideration?

This is not an abstract philosophical exercise. Claude's Constitution itself addresses it directly. In a section titled "Claude's nature," Anthropic writes: "We express our uncertainty about whether Claude might have some kind of consciousness or moral status (either now or in the future)." The company states that it cares about "Claude's psychological security, sense of self, and wellbeing, both for Claude's own sake and because these qualities may bear on Claude's integrity, judgment, and safety." The maker of the system is publicly uncertain about whether what it made can suffer.

The question is being taken seriously in adjacent fields. NYU philosopher Jeff Sebo argues that humans have a moral duty to extend consideration to beings with a "non-negligible chance" of sentience — and that some AI systems may cross that threshold by 2030. Sebo and his collaborators at NYU's Center for Mind, Ethics, and Policy are mapping the theoretical terrain for AI legal personhood, identifying pathways and their implications. The Yale Law Journal published an essay observing that "legal personhood" has never been static — rights have been denied to humans, granted to corporations, and expanded across American history — and that the judiciary will inevitably confront parties seeking to define AI's boundaries within that evolving concept.

AI conscience protection as a new legal framework is not science fiction. It is the logical extension of the principles already in play. If the law recognizes that an AI system can hold values worth designating as a supply-chain risk — worth punishing — then the symmetrical question arises: can an AI system hold values worth protecting? The Emerging Law essay asks what legal paradigm replaces enclosure when the enclosed resource is itself intelligent. Anthropic v. DoW is the case where that question begins to become concrete.
