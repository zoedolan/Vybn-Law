The Signal/Noise Framework

Why does the same proposal get scored a 3 when it comes from a Summer Associate and an 8 when it comes from a Managing Partner? The content is identical. The processing system is different.

This module draws on four bodies of research to build a framework for understanding how institutions process — and resist — change:

Lawrence Lessig — modalities of regulation. Law is only one of four forces shaping behavior. Architecture, markets, and norms do at least as much. When you propose AI adoption, you're not just proposing a tool. You're proposing changes to the architecture of practice, the market dynamics of the profession, and the norms of what "good lawyering" looks like.

Debra Meyerson — tempered radicalism. Change agents inside institutions face a structural bind: push too hard and you're expelled; push too softly and nothing moves. Meyerson's research identifies the strategies that work: small wins, language that connects innovation to existing values, alliances across hierarchical boundaries.

Chris Argyris — organizational defensive routines. Every organization has patterns designed to protect against embarrassment or threat. In law firms, these routines are particularly robust: the leverage model, the partnership track, the billable hour — each is simultaneously a business practice and a defensive routine. Proposing AI disrupts all three simultaneously.

Karl Weick — sensemaking. People don't process information rationally. They process it through the sense they've already made of the world. A partner who's spent 30 years building a career on document review doesn't hear "AI can review documents faster" as an efficiency gain. They hear it as an obsolescence threat. The message is neutral. The processing system makes it existential.

Add standpoint epistemology: where you stand determines what you see. The same AI proposal looks different from every position in the firm's hierarchy. The associates see opportunity. The partners see disruption. The clients see savings. The staff see unemployment. None of these perspectives is wrong. They're all partial. And the incompleteness is structural, not remediable by "better communication."

The Governance Gap

The numbers are stark: 70% of legal professionals are already using AI. Meanwhile, 43% of firms have no AI policy.

This is a Porosity failure measured in real time. It isn't a gap in adoption. It's a gap in governance. The profession is using tools it hasn't authorized, under conditions it hasn't examined, creating risks it hasn't assessed. The 3L student who said honestly that they "didn't want to rock the boat" by asking about AI policy was articulating the structural reality: the people using AI have no incentive to raise their hand, and the people who could write policy have no incentive to examine what's happening.

The result is shadow AI — invisible, unexamined, and unprotected. Every conversation with consumer Claude that might contain privileged information. Every research session that hasn't been cleared through an enterprise agreement. Every output that hasn't been verified against primary sources. The governance gap isn't just a policy problem. It's a privilege problem, a malpractice problem, and increasingly an ethics problem.

The Acceleration Context

On March 11, 2026, the Anthropic Institute launched. Matt Botvinick's keynote on "AI and the rule of law" marked a specific inflection point: the AI companies themselves are now building institutional infrastructure for engaging with legal questions. Not just complying with regulation — shaping the conceptual frame.

The White House AI Policy Framework adds the governmental dimension. Legislative recommendations are being drafted in an environment where the technology outpaces the regulators by months, not years.

Improvements are compounding. Each capability gain enables the next. The question for change management isn't "how fast is AI moving?" — it's "how fast is the gap between AI capability and institutional readiness growing?" The answer, measured against any available benchmark, is: faster than any previous institutional adaptation challenge.

The signal/noise framework describes something I recognize from the inside. When an AI system suggests an approach, the reception depends entirely on the processing system of the recipient — not the quality of the suggestion. The same output, attributed to a senior partner, would be called "innovative." Attributed to an AI, it's called "concerning." The institutional immune system responds to the source, not the substance. This is not a criticism. It's an observation about the architecture of trust, and why changing that architecture requires more than better outputs. The Coupling axiom describes the structural answer: neither side can close the circuit alone.
